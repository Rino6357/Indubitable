He heaved for breath as the six-legged construct gained on him. Its legs were connected to a conical base patterned with strange runes, cutting off at a roughly cylindrical head where a single eye rested. A malice corrupted relic of ancient technology. His balance was wavering, and every next stride seemed to be his last, but he kept running. His brother would’ve been able to outrun this thing. He had always been better at tag. Yes, yes… This was like a game of tag. A game of tag where you’d die once you’re caught.

Beneath the sound of his gasps for air, he could hear the inner mechanisms of the monster growing louder. They were all supposed to be in the central fields—they loved the open plains, so why was this one here? No. No time for questions. He needed to keep his balance. If he fell, it’d be all over.

Suddenly, he heard a rapid build up of screeching sounds, and without thinking, he looked back. The beast was nearly upon him, and his legs no longer moved. From its eye a thin line of light traced towards his chest, where there shone a red circle. He’d seen this before. He remembered what came after. It was over. Why couldn’t he move his legs?

As the screeching reached its climax, a whistle sounded in the air and the guardian recoiled backward, its laser disappearing from his chest. Huh? Squinting, he saw something lodged in the mechanical eye. Seizing the distraction, breath exited his lungs after what felt like an eternity of holding it. The monster turned away from him toward a figure in the distance. It was an archer on horseback, too far for a good look. What skill! The construct, arrow in eye, moved rapidly towards the man. He was saved! He could run back to the village, and the archer could outrun the beast to safety. Yes!

But the archer did not move. Why wasn’t he running for his life? Was he insane? He moved to call for the man, but his fear would not let him open his mouth. His savior would be killed. His muscles screamed in pain as the fatigue caught up to him, and he fell to his knees, unable to do anything but watch.

As the construct crept closer, the man unmounted his horse. He holstered his bow on his back, unsheathing a sword that seemed to glow in the distance. The construct halted before him, its laser focused on the new adversary. It was unbelievable. What sane person would attack one of these monsters, let alone face one head on? The man, unbothered, planted his feet on the ground and raised his sword. Green embers sparked around him, and he spun.

Lightning fell from the clear sky. He couldn’t believe what he was seeing. Despite his fatigue, he scrambled to get a closer look. The man was clad in a vibrant blue, the shade of the champions, and his hair golden in his cowl. Impossible. It had been a century since the champions had lost their lives fighting the calamity. Yet here this man was…

The man threw a boomerang from his back toward the beast, closing the distance between him and the stunned beast. Leaping into the air, he put his shield beneath his feet. The boomerang destroyed one of the construct’s legs as it shot back towards the man, returning into the air as its master ducked under it. As he landed onto the body of the beast, the boomerang destroyed another leg, creating an impossible updraft. The construct was sent flying up, the man using his shield to shoot himself higher in the air.

He gaped. Who was this man? Could this even be a man?

The boomerang spiraled following the ascent, destroying a leg in each revolution. Sheathing his sword behind him, the man equipped his bow.

…

…

Time slowed down as he knocked his arrow. This high, he could see the land in its entirety. The broken castle, the smoldering mountain, the beautiful plains. This land was once so full of life. He almost could not believe that this was the same kingdom that he had lived in so long ago.

He felt his gloved hand graze his cheek, and he shifted his focus onto the Guardian. Machines created for the purpose of defending the royal family, now malice corrupted death machines that destroyed anything in their path. His failure against them had caused the lives of so many. Too many. How could they ever forgive him? How could he ever forgive himself?

The moment the arrow left his hand, the flow of time returned. The arrow shot from his bow as if commanded by the very wind, splitting the previous arrow as it made its mark.

…

…

The construct recoiled, wreathing in the air. A boom echoed in the planes as it exploded smokily in the air, dropping a rain of parts. The warrior, using some sort of cloth, slowed his fall and landed unscathed. The champion picked himself up and led his horse towards the remnants of the beast, picking some of them and storing them in his saddlebags. He called out to him, asking him for his name. He needed to know who this man was, if he was a man at all. The champion looked back, wavered for a second, and lifted his cowl.

He stared in shock. Impossible! He must be dreaming. Has the time finally come? He tried to chase after him, but his face fell into the ground. Damn those legs!

The champion turned, mounted his steed, and galloped away.
